Resilience patterns: circuit breakers, bulkheads, timeouts

Hard resilience fault-tolerance distributed-systems

The idea in one paragraph

In distributed systems, failures are not exceptional — they are the steady state. Resilience patterns are the small set of defensive techniques that turn one slow dependency into a contained nuisance instead of a system-wide outage. The three load-bearing primitives are timeouts (bound how long you wait), circuit breakers (stop calling something that is broken), and bulkheads (isolate resource pools so one failing path can't drown the others). Add retries with backoff, hedging, and load shedding, and you have most of what production needs.

Why it matters

The classic failure mode is cascading collapse: dependency D slows from 50ms to 5s, every caller's threadpool fills with stuck requests, callers' callers time out, and now the whole graph is yellow. None of those services "broke" — they were too patient. Resilience patterns are the explicit budget that says "I will wait at most X, then I will fail fast and shed load." Fail-fast is counter-intuitive but it is the only way a healthy service stays healthy when an unhealthy neighbor is still answering.

Real-world examples

  • Netflix Hystrix / resilience4j: popularized circuit breakers with thread-pool bulkheads per dependency.
  • Envoy / Istio: implement outlier detection, retry budgets, and per-upstream connection pools at the proxy.
  • AWS SDKs: ship adaptive retry mode with token-bucket-based backoff to avoid retry storms.
  • Google SRE book: describes deadline propagation across RPC chains so every hop respects the original budget.

The mental model

caller breaker dependency |---req-->| CLOSED |---req-->| (healthy: pass through, count failures) |<--ok----| |<--ok----| |---req-->| OPEN | short-circuit (>=N fails in window: refuse for cool-down) |<--err---| | no call |---req-->| HALF-OPEN|---probe->| (after cool-down: let 1 in, then close or re-open) |<--ok----| |<--ok-----| bulkhead view: [pool A: 20 threads] --> dep-A (slow dep cannot starve pool B) [pool B: 20 threads] --> dep-B [pool C: 10 threads] --> dep-C

The breaker has three states. Closed = normal traffic, but track failure rate. Cross a threshold (say 50% errors over the last 20 calls) and trip to Open: every call returns immediately with a fallback. After a cool-down (say 30s), move to Half-Open and let a few probes through. If they succeed, close again; if they fail, re-open. Bulkheads sit underneath: each downstream gets its own threadpool, semaphore, or connection pool, so a stuck dependency cannot consume all the caller's capacity.

Choosing timeouts and budgets

Pick timeouts from the latency distribution, not from gut feel. A reasonable default is p99 of the dependency × 1.5, capped by the caller's own deadline. Then propagate the deadline: if the user-facing request has 800ms left and you call service B, B should be told it has 800ms left, and B's own calls inherit whatever remains. Without deadline propagation, retries multiply: a 3-deep call chain with 2 retries each is 27 attempts in the worst case. Add a retry budget (e.g., "retries may not exceed 10% of normal traffic") so you never amplify a brownout.

Common mistakes

  • Infinite or default timeouts. Most HTTP clients default to "wait forever." Always set one explicitly.
  • Retrying non-idempotent writes. A retry on a POST that already succeeded creates duplicates. Use idempotency keys.
  • One global threadpool. Without bulkheads, one slow dependency starves every other code path in the process.
  • Synchronized retries. Without jitter, all clients retry at the same instant and DDoS the recovering service.
  • Breaker thresholds based on count, not rate. "5 failures" trips instantly under load; use rate over a sliding window.
  • No fallback story. A breaker that returns 500 on open is just faster failure. Decide: cached value, default, degraded UX, or queued for later.

Try it yourself

Question: Your checkout service calls a fraud-scoring service whose p50 is 40ms but p99 is 4s and occasionally times out. Checkout itself has an 800ms SLA. Design the resilience layer: what timeout, what breaker thresholds, what fallback when fraud is unavailable, and how do you keep peak retry load below 10% of normal?
Hint: Combine a tight per-call timeout (~250ms), a per-dependency bulkhead, a breaker that opens on >30% errors over 30s, and a fallback that ships the order to an async review queue when fraud is open. Use a token-bucket retry budget.
