CAP theorem and PACELC

Easy cap pacelc consistency

The idea in one paragraph

CAP says: in a distributed system that experiences a network Partition, you must choose between Consistency (every read sees the latest write) and Availability (every request gets a non-error response). PACELC adds the part everyone forgets — even when there's no partition (E for "else"), you still trade Latency against Consistency. So a real system has two stances: how it behaves under partition, and how it behaves on a sunny day.

Why it matters

Vendors love to claim "highly available and strongly consistent". CAP forces you to ask the awkward follow-up: what happens during a network split? PACELC forces the next one: what does normal-day latency cost in consistency? Together they give you a vocabulary to compare DynamoDB, Spanner, MongoDB, Cassandra, and your home-grown service honestly.

Real-world examples

  • DynamoDB: PA/EL — sacrifices consistency under partition and on the happy path for low latency (eventually consistent reads by default).
  • Spanner: PC/EC — refuses writes during partition rather than diverge, and pays latency on every write for strong consistency.
  • MongoDB (default): PA/EC — primary stays available; reads from primary are consistent on the happy path.
  • Cassandra: PA/EL — tunable, but defaults favor availability and low latency over strong reads.

The mental model

IF partition? | +---------+----------+ | | YES (P) NO (E - "else") | | pick C or A pick L or C | | CP: refuse writes EL: low-latency, weak read AP: serve, diverge EC: strong read, more RTTs

Read PACELC as a two-letter label: PA/EL means "AP under partition, prefer Latency in normal operation." The first letter is the partition stance, the third is the no-partition stance. Most "NoSQL" systems are PA/EL; most "NewSQL" systems (Spanner, CockroachDB) are PC/EC.

The nuance CAP gets wrong

CAP is a coarse pop-science framing. In reality, "consistency" means linearizability (a very strong property), and "availability" means every non-failing node responds — a stricter definition than the SLA you actually care about. Most systems live on a spectrum: bounded staleness, read-your-writes, monotonic reads. Use CAP to start the conversation, not to end it.

Common mistakes

  • Thinking you can pick "all three" — under a real partition, you genuinely can't.
  • Confusing "available" (responds at all) with "low latency" — they're different axes; that's why PACELC exists.
  • Calling a system "CA" — partitions will happen; you don't get to opt out of P.
  • Treating consistency as a single dial; per-operation tuning (DynamoDB strong reads, Cassandra QUORUM) is the norm.

Try it yourself

Question: You're designing a global shopping cart. A network partition splits EU and US data centers. Do you allow writes on both sides (and merge later) or block one?
Hint: Think about what's worse for users — losing edits or showing stale state. The answer can differ for the cart vs. the checkout step.
