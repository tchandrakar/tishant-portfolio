Consistency models: strong, eventual, causal, RYW, monotonic

Easy consistency distributed-systems

The idea in one paragraph

A consistency model is a contract between a storage system and its clients about what reads are allowed to return after a sequence of writes. Strong consistency promises every read sees the latest write; eventual consistency promises only that, given enough quiet time, replicas converge. In between sit a family of weaker but useful guarantees — read-your-writes, monotonic reads, causal — that fix specific surprises without paying full coordination cost.

Why it matters

Pick too strong a model and you pay in latency, availability, and dollars. Pick too weak and your users see disappearing posts, time-traveling counters, and "I just clicked save and it's gone." Most production bugs in distributed apps are consistency-model mismatches: a developer assumed strong, the database delivered eventual, and the gap showed up in production at 2 AM.

The five models, ranked from strong to weak

  • Strong (linearizable): reads see the most recent committed write, globally. Behaves as if there were one copy. Expensive — needs consensus.
  • Causal: if write A happened-before write B (e.g., a reply to a post), every replica that sees B also sees A. Independent writes can appear in any order.
  • Read-your-writes (RYW): after you write, your subsequent reads from the same session see your write. Other users may not yet.
  • Monotonic reads: once you've read a value, you'll never read an older one. No time travel.
  • Eventual: if writes stop, all replicas converge. Until then, anything goes.

Real-world examples

  • Spanner / etcd / Zookeeper: linearizable — used for configuration, leases, metadata.
  • DynamoDB strong-read mode: linearizable per key (one extra hop, pay 2x RCU).
  • S3 (since 2020): strong read-after-write on objects.
  • Cassandra QUORUM: approximates strong reads via R+W>N.
  • Social-media feeds: usually eventual, often patched with RYW so you see your own post immediately.

The mental model

STRONG CAUSAL RYW MONOTONIC EVENTUAL ---------------------------------------------------------------- global respects my session no going converges total happens- sees my backwards eventually order before writes ---------------------------------------------------------------- <-- more coordination, higher latency / less availability --> <-------- weaker contract, cheaper, more available ------->

Each step right loosens the contract and buys availability or latency. Crucially, the weaker models are composable: a session that gets RYW + monotonic reads + causal feels almost-strong to a single user, while letting different users see slightly different states.

Picking a model — a cheat sheet

  • Money / inventory / locks: linearizable. Don't be clever.
  • User profile, posts, comments: RYW + causal usually nails the "feels right" bar.
  • Likes / view counts / analytics: eventual is fine; users won't notice a 2-second drift.
  • Chat / collaborative docs: causal at minimum, often plus CRDTs for merge.

Common mistakes

  • Assuming "we replicate synchronously" means strong — it doesn't, unless you also coordinate writes.
  • Promising eventual to users who do read-modify-write loops; lost updates are guaranteed without versioning.
  • Mixing replicas in one request — round-robin reads to async replicas violate monotonic reads.
  • Forgetting that "session" guarantees vanish when the user reconnects to a different node.
