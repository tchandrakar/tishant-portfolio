Pub-sub patterns: fanout, topic, exchange

Medium pubsub fanout routing

The idea in one paragraph

Pub-sub flips the queue model on its head: producers don't know who consumes, and consumers don't know who produced. The broker sits in between with a routing rule. Three rules cover 90% of real systems — fanout (everyone gets every message), topic (subscribers filter by a key or pattern), and direct/exchange (explicit binding from a routing key to a queue). Knowing which one to reach for is the difference between a clean fan-out architecture and a tangle of point-to-point HTTP calls.

Why it matters

Once you have more than two services that care about the same domain event ("order placed", "user signed up"), you need pub-sub or you're back to the N-squared integration mess. The pattern you pick determines who can subscribe later without redeploying producers, how filtering happens, and whether a slow consumer can stall everyone else.

Real-world examples

  • Fanout: A "user.deleted" event broadcast to billing, search, audit, and email-unsubscribe — each service maintains its own queue from the same exchange.
  • Topic: Logs routed by service.environment.severity; a dashboard subscribes to *.prod.error while a per-service team binds to checkout.#.
  • Direct/exchange: RabbitMQ direct exchange routing job types — image.resize goes to GPU workers, email.send goes to SMTP workers, both from one producer.

The mental model

fanout topic direct ┌─────┐ ┌─────────────┐ ┌─────────┐ │ P │ │ P key=... │ │ P key=A│ └──┬──┘ └──────┬──────┘ └────┬────┘ │ everyone │ pattern match │ exact match ┌─┴─┬─┐ ┌──────┼──────┐ ┌─────┴────┐ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ ▼ Q1 Q2 Q3 order.* *.eu audit.# Q-A (no Q-B)

Fanout copies blindly. Topic uses wildcards (* = one segment, # = zero+ segments) to pull a slice. Direct is a hash-map lookup from key to queue.

Designing the routing key

  • Hierarchical, dotted, low-cardinality on the left: domain.action.region — left-to-right wildcards work better when the broadest dimension is leftmost.
  • Don't put user IDs in the key. You'll create a binding explosion. Use payload fields and per-consumer filtering for that.
  • Version your topics. order.placed.v1 lets you ship a v2 alongside without breaking subscribers.

Failure & backpressure

Pub-sub doesn't eliminate slow consumers — it isolates them. Each subscriber gets its own queue, so one stuck consumer fills its queue without blocking siblings. But the broker's disk is finite: set per-queue length limits, dead-letter exchanges for poison messages, and a TTL so abandoned queues don't grow forever. Fanout is especially dangerous here — N consumers means N copies, so a 1KB event becomes NKB of storage.

Common mistakes

  • Using fanout when you actually want a topic — every service receives events it doesn't care about and has to filter in code.
  • Encoding business logic in routing keys (order.amount.>100). Routing keys are addresses, not predicates.
  • Sharing one queue across consumer types. You then can't scale image-resize workers independently of email workers.
  • Forgetting that pub-sub is at-least-once. Subscribers must be idempotent or you'll double-process.

Try it yourself

Question: A travel site emits booking.created. Five services care: payments, inventory, email confirmation, analytics, fraud. Inventory only cares about flights, fraud only cares about bookings > $5000. Sketch the exchange + queue + binding topology.
Hint: Use a topic exchange. Some bindings are routing-key based (booking.flight.*); the dollar threshold doesn't belong in the key — push that to a consumer-side filter or a dedicated fraud-evaluator queue.
