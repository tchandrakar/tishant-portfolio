Design Netflix (VOD at planetary scale)

Hard video-streaming cdn recommendation

1. Problem statement

Design a global video-on-demand service that streams a curated catalog of ~17,000 titles to roughly 250 million subscribers across 190+ countries. Netflix accounts for ~15% of global internet downstream traffic at peak, delivers about 1 billion hours of viewing per week, and must keep startup latency under two seconds while maintaining 99.99% playback availability. The system spans content ingest, multi-codec transcoding, a private CDN (Open Connect), personalization, billing, and viewing telemetry.

2. Functional requirements

  • Browse a personalized home page with rows of titles per profile.
  • Search the catalog by title, actor, genre.
  • Start, pause, resume, and seek within any title from any device.
  • Sync watch progress across devices within seconds.
  • Support offline downloads for mobile profiles.
  • Recommend "next title" based on viewing history and similar users.

3. Non-functional requirements

  • Latency: playback start < 2 s p95; trick-play seek < 500 ms.
  • Scale: 250 M MAU, ~100 M concurrent peak (e.g., a tentpole release), 1 B viewing hours/week.
  • Availability: 99.99% for playback, 99.9% for control plane.
  • Throughput: tens of Tbps egress at peak from edge caches.
  • Durability: 11 nines for master mezzanines.

4. Capacity estimation

  • QPS: 100 M concurrent / 30 s heartbeat ≈ 3.3 M control-plane RPS; manifest fetches add another 2 M RPS.
  • Storage: 17 K titles × 50 encodes (codec × bitrate × HDR variants) × ~5 GB avg ≈ 4 PB master + 200 PB across regional caches.
  • Bandwidth: avg bitrate 5 Mbps × 100 M concurrent = 500 Tbps theoretical; actual peak ~200 Tbps thanks to ABR shaping.
  • Telemetry: 100 M concurrent × 1 event/2 s = 50 M events/s into Kafka.

6. High-level architecture

[Client TVs/Mobile] │ manifest + segments ▼ [Open Connect Appliance @ ISP] ── miss ──► [Regional Fill Tier] ── miss ──► [S3 Origin] │ │ control plane (HTTPS) ▼ [AWS Edge / Zuul GW] → [API Aggregator] → [Playback Svc] → [License/DRM] ├──► [Personalization Svc] ← [Cassandra: viewer state] ├──► [Catalog Svc] ← [EVCache + Cassandra] └──► [Search (Elasticsearch)] │ [Kafka: play-events] → [Flink] → [S3 + Iceberg] → [Reco training]

Clients first hit AWS for auth, manifest URLs, and personalization. Video bytes never traverse AWS — they come from Open Connect appliances (OCAs) embedded inside ISP networks, which is what makes the unit economics work at this scale.

7. Data model & storage

7.1 Viewer state (Cassandra)

CREATE TABLE viewer_progress (
  profile_id uuid,
  title_id   uuid,
  position_ms bigint,
  updated_at timestamp,
  device_id text,
  PRIMARY KEY (profile_id, title_id)
);

7.2 Why Cassandra?

Multi-region active-active with tunable consistency (LOCAL_QUORUM for writes, ONE for reads). DDIA Ch. 5 explains why leaderless replication wins for write-heavy, geographically distributed workloads where last-write-wins on a per-row basis is acceptable.

7.3 Catalog & assets

Title metadata in MySQL (source of truth, low write rate) → CDC to Cassandra/EVCache for read scale. Encoded video sits in S3 (mezzanines) and is replicated to OCAs via the Open Connect proactive fill protocol nightly during off-peak hours.

8. Detailed component design

8.1 Open Connect (CDN)

Custom FreeBSD appliances with NVMe and ~200 Gbps NICs, deployed inside ISPs. Each OCA stores the most-popular ~80% of the catalog for its locale. Cache fill is predictive — Netflix knows viewing patterns and pushes content before peak hours rather than relying on demand-driven fill, which avoids origin thundering herds.

8.2 Playback service

Issues a signed manifest pointing to the closest healthy OCA based on client IP → ISP → OCA mapping. DRM keys (Widevine/PlayReady/FairPlay) are returned via a separate license server with short TTLs.

8.3 Recommendations

Two-stage: candidate generation via collaborative filtering + content embeddings (matrix factorization, then graph-based ANN), followed by a per-row ranking model (LambdaMART). Features come from a feature store fed by Flink jobs reading the Kafka play-event stream.

9. Scaling & bottlenecks

  • Tentpole releases (e.g., new season drop): pre-warm OCAs globally; rate-limit clients with progressive backoff.
  • Regional AWS outage: Netflix's Chaos Monkey + region failover lets the control plane fall over to two surviving regions in < 7 minutes; playback already in progress continues because it reads from OCAs.
  • Hot title: consistent-hashed within an OCA cluster + tier-2 regional fill so a single appliance never melts.
  • Cassandra hot partitions: bucket viewer_progress by (profile_id, day_bucket) for high-velocity profiles.

12. Talking points for the interviewer

  • "I'd separate control plane (AWS) from data plane (OCAs) on day one — it's the single decision that makes Netflix economics work."
  • "For a tentpole launch, the bottleneck is not encoding or storage, it's last-mile ISP capacity, so I'd lean on predictive fill 24 hours ahead."
  • "The hardest part is recommendation freshness — viewing patterns shift hourly but model retraining is daily; my mitigation is a real-time re-ranker on top of cached candidates."
