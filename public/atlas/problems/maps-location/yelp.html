Design Yelp / nearby places review platform

Hard maps-location search recommendation

1. Problem statement

Build a location-based discovery platform: users open the app, see ranked restaurants/businesses within walking distance, filter by cuisine/rating/price, read 250 M+ reviews, post their own with photos, and reserve a table. Read-heavy (≈50:1 read:write), latency-sensitive on the home feed (<300 ms), and search relevance must blend distance, rating, recency, and personalisation.

2. Functional requirements

  • "Nearby" feed: top-K businesses within radius r of (lat, lng) with filters.
  • Full-text + geo search ("ramen near me, open now, $$").
  • Business detail: photos, hours, menu, 5-star rating, paginated reviews.
  • Write reviews + photos; verified-check-in via GPS proximity.
  • Reservations integration (OpenTable-style slots).
  • Owner portal: claim listing, respond to reviews, run promoted ads.

3. Non-functional requirements

  • Latency: nearby feed p99 < 300 ms; search p99 < 400 ms.
  • Scale: 250 M MAU, 80 M DAU, 5 M businesses, 250 M reviews, 1 B photos.
  • QPS: 60 K rps search avg, 200 K peak; 500 rps review writes.
  • Availability: 99.95%; reviews durable (no loss).
  • Freshness: new review visible < 60 s; rating recompute < 5 min.

4. Capacity estimation

  • Reviews: 250 M × 1 KB text + 5 photos × 200 KB ≈ 250 TB text + 1 PB photos.
  • Geo-index: 5 M businesses × 200 B = 1 GB — fully RAM-resident per shard.
  • Read fan-out: home feed = 1 geo query + 50 business detail lookups (cached).
  • Cache: hottest 100 K businesses × 5 KB = 500 MB Redis (LFU).
  • Photo CDN egress: 80 M DAU × 30 photo views × 80 KB = 200 TB/day.

6. High-level architecture

[Mobile] ─▶ [API GW + Rate Limit] ─▶ [Feed Svc] ──▶ [Geo Index: S2/Geohash, RAM] │ │ ├─▶ [Search: Elasticsearch cluster] ├─▶ [Business Svc] ─▶ [MySQL (sharded by biz_id)] ├─▶ [Review Svc] ─▶ [Cassandra reviews table] └─▶ [Reco Svc] ─▶ [Feature Store + LightGBM] ▲ [Photo Upload] ─▶ [S3] ─▶ [Lambda: thumbnails] ─▶ [CloudFront CDN]──┘ [Review Write] ─▶ [Kafka] ─▶ [Flink: rating rollup, spam filter] ─▶ [MySQL biz.stats]

Feed flow: client sends (lat,lng) → Feed Svc queries the in-memory geo index for candidate biz_ids within radius → fetches details from Redis (fallback MySQL) → ranks via Reco Svc → returns. Writes go through Kafka so rating rollups, spam classification, and search index updates are async but eventually consistent within a minute.

7. Data model & storage

7.1 Business (MySQL, sharded by biz_id)

CREATE TABLE businesses (
  biz_id BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  name VARCHAR(200), category_ids JSON,
  lat DECIMAL(9,6), lng DECIMAL(9,6),
  geohash9 CHAR(9), s2_cell BIGINT,
  avg_rating FLOAT, review_count INT,
  hours JSON, price_tier TINYINT
) /* secondary idx on geohash9 */;

7.2 Reviews (Cassandra)

CREATE TABLE reviews_by_biz (
  biz_id bigint, created_at timeuuid,
  user_id bigint, stars tinyint, body text, photo_ids list<text>,
  PRIMARY KEY (biz_id, created_at)
) WITH CLUSTERING ORDER BY (created_at DESC);

7.3 Geo index (custom, in-RAM)

S2 cell-id at level 13 (≈1 km²) → bucket of biz_ids, sorted by popularity. Backed by snapshot from MySQL every 10 min; deltas via Kafka tail. Shards by S2 face (6 shards) — each <200 MB.

7.4 Why this mix?

MySQL: sane joins for owner CRUD; sharded by biz_id avoids cross-shard reads on detail page. Cassandra: review write throughput + clustering by created_at for cheap pagination. Custom geo index: ES geo-distance queries hit ~30 ms p99, the in-RAM S2 index hits 2 ms — matters for the home feed.

8. Detailed component design

8.1 Geo lookup

Compute caller's S2 cell at level 13; expand to covering cells of radius r (typically 9 cells); union of candidate lists; filter by exact haversine. For dense urban cells we cap candidates at 500 and let the ranker do the rest.

8.2 Ranking (DDIA Ch. 11 stream-derived features)

LightGBM with features: distance, log(review_count), Wilson-lower-bound rating, "open now" boolean, personal click history, time-of-day match, promoted-bid (sponsored slot capped at 1 of top 5).

8.3 Spam & review integrity

Async pipeline: Kafka → Flink → text classifier + IP/device velocity → quarantine queue → human ops for borderline. Verified-check-in (GPS within 100 m of biz at time of review) gives a "verified" badge boosting trust score.

8.4 Photo pipeline

Direct-to-S3 upload via signed URL → S3 event → Lambda generates 240/480/1080 thumbnails → CDN. EXIF stripped server-side for privacy.

9. Scaling & bottlenecks

  • Tourist hot spots (Times Square): single S2 cell with 8 K businesses — pre-rank by popularity into a "hot cell" Redis set.
  • Rating recompute thundering herd: debounce per biz_id (1 update / 30 s) — DDIA Ch. 11 windowed aggregation.
  • Long-tail reviews: Cassandra's clustering by created_at prevents wide rows by time-bucketing on year.
  • Search relevance drift: nightly offline relevance evaluation against held-out clicks.

13. Tech stack & rationale

Every technology choice in this design exists for a concrete reason.

TechnologyWhy we chose it (for THIS problem)Alternatives considered
S2 cells (level 13, ~1 km²) The home feed needs 2 ms p99 candidate retrieval; S2 gives equal-area cells globally so a 2 km radius lookup reliably touches ~9 cells whether you're in Tokyo or Reykjavik. Equal-area matters because Yelp's 5 M businesses are globally distributed and we don't want lat-distorted relevance. Geohash (rectangle distortion near poles); H3 (great for hex coverage but radius queries need extra ring math); ES geo_distance (~30 ms p99, 15× slower).
In-RAM custom geo index (sharded by S2 face) 5 M businesses × 200 B = 1 GB fits trivially in RAM per shard, giving 2 ms lookups vs 30 ms in ES. With 6 S2 face shards under 200 MB each, snapshot/restore takes seconds and we sustain 200 K rps peak without ES tax. Elasticsearch geo (~30 ms p99, expensive licensing); PostGIS (~5-20 ms, write-bottlenecked); Redis GEO (good for v1 but no attribute filters).
MySQL (sharded by biz_id) Owner CRUD (claim listing, edit hours, respond to reviews) needs joins and transactions across business + photos + reservations. Sharding by biz_id keeps the detail page a single-shard read, which is the hot path served from Redis 95% of the time anyway. PostgreSQL (similar — chose MySQL for ops familiarity); DynamoDB (no joins, painful for owner portal); Spanner (overkill, expensive).
Cassandra (reviews) 250 M reviews growing at 500 wps need linear write scaling, and "load reviews newest-first" is a clustering-key range scan over (biz_id, created_at DESC). Cassandra nails this pattern; durability via tunable consistency QUORUM matches our "no review loss" SLA. MySQL (review row count would dwarf business shards); DynamoDB (10 KB item limit awkward for long reviews + photos); ScyllaDB (similar; chose Cassandra for ops maturity).
Elasticsearch (full-text + geo search) "Ramen near me, open now, $$" is a multi-field query: text (ngrams), geo (distance filter), category, hours, price tier. ES BM25 + geo_distance + custom function_score handles this in 50-100 ms; building it ourselves with retrieval-time blending would take a team a year. Solr (smaller talent pool); Algolia (cost prohibitive at 60 K rps); Vespa (fewer hosted options); Postgres FTS (no fuzzy + facet ranking).
Kafka + Flink (rating rollup, spam, search-index sync) 500 review wps with 1-min freshness on rating recompute and spam classification needs a stream backbone. Flink's keyed state debounces per-biz_id rollups (1 update/30 s) and integrates ML scoring inline, hitting the 5-min staleness SLA without overwhelming MySQL with rating writes. Spark Streaming (micro-batch latency); Kinesis Firehose (no stateful ops); cron-based recompute (5-min cron = 5-min freshness floor with herd issues).
Redis (hot-business cache, hot-cell pre-rank) Hottest 100 K businesses × 5 KB = 500 MB easily fits one Redis shard. LFU eviction + sub-ms reads serve 80% of detail page lookups. For tourist hotspots (Times Square's 8 K-business cell), we maintain a sorted set pre-ranked by popularity to skip per-request ranking entirely. Memcached (no sorted sets); local in-process LRU (no cross-pod sharing); DynamoDB DAX (vendor lock-in, slower).
S3 + CloudFront + Lambda (photo pipeline) 1 B photos at 200 KB each = 200 TB; 80 M DAU × 30 photo views = 200 TB/day egress. S3 stores cheap, signed-URL direct upload bypasses our app servers, Lambda generates 240/480/1080 thumbnails on the S3 event, and CloudFront absorbs the egress at edge POPs. Self-hosted Ceph (ops nightmare at PB); GCS+Cloud Functions (similar — we're on AWS); imgix (great but cost at 200 TB/day).
LightGBM (re-ranking) Retrieval gives top-500 candidates from S2; ranking blends distance, log(review_count), Wilson-lower-bound rating, "open now", click priors, and bid in <5 ms. Tree models handle the heterogeneous feature mix and explain promoted-bid placement for transparency. Deep ranker (50 ms — too slow for feed); pure rule-based (no personalisation); XGBoost (similar perf, LightGBM is 2× faster training on click logs).
Wilson lower bound (rating ranking) A 5.0-stars-from-2-reviews business shouldn't outrank a 4.6-stars-from-2000-reviews place. Wilson lower bound on a Bernoulli interval gives a confidence-aware score that punishes low-sample averages — directly defending against "buy 5 fake 5-star reviews" attacks. Plain mean (rewards thin samples); Bayesian average with global prior (similar but more tunable knobs to defend); IMDb formula (effectively a special case of Bayesian).
Geohash9 secondary index on businesses For batch jobs ("export all bay-area businesses for ML training"), a geohash9 column with a B-tree index lets MySQL serve city-bbox queries at ~150 ms without round-tripping through S2. Different tool for different workload than the live S2 in-RAM index. S2 cell column (works but PostGIS-style queries need int range gymnastics); PostGIS migration (operational lift not justified for batch path).
GPS-verified check-in (review trust signal) Verified check-ins (GPS within 100 m at review time) cut fake-review noise by ~70% and feed the spam classifier as a strong free feature. At 500 wps and a clamoring fake-review economy, cheap trust signals beat expensive ML. Photo EXIF geotag check (easily faked); IP geolocation (carrier-grade NAT obscures); manual moderation only (doesn't scale).
