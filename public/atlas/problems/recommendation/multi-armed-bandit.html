Design Multi-armed Bandit Recommender (exploration vs exploitation, Thompson)

Hard recommendation ad-tech

1. Problem statement

Design a multi-armed bandit (MAB) layer for a recommender / ad-tech platform that balances exploration and exploitation across thousands of arms (creatives, items, modules). Use Thompson Sampling and contextual bandits (LinUCB / contextual TS) to adapt within minutes to changing reward distributions. Targets: 1B users, 580K decisions/sec, sub-100ms decision latency, posterior updates within 60 seconds of feedback.

2. Functional requirements

  • Pick an arm per request given context (user features, surface).
  • Update posterior parameters from reward signals (click, conversion).
  • Support both stationary (Beta-Bernoulli) and contextual (LinUCB) bandits.
  • Allow operators to add/remove arms with warm-start priors.
  • Per-user dedupe so the same user isn't served the same exploratory arm too often.
  • Off-policy evaluation (IPS / DR) for new policies before launch.

3. Non-functional requirements

  • Latency: arm selection p99 under 10 ms; with retrieval, total under 100 ms.
  • Scale: 580K decisions/sec; 10K-100K active arms per surface.
  • Update lag: rewards integrated into posterior within 60 seconds.
  • Availability: 99.95%; degrade to greedy on the prior mean if posterior store is down.

4. Capacity estimation

  • Posterior state: 100K arms * 200 bytes (Beta(alpha,beta) + LinUCB matrix sketch) = 20 MB per surface; 1000 surfaces = 20 GB total. Fits in Redis.
  • Reward events: 580K decisions/sec * 0.05 reward rate = 29K reward events/sec. 200 bytes each = 6 MB/sec into Kafka.
  • Decision logging (for off-policy eval): 580K * 500 bytes = 290 MB/sec, 25 TB/day.
  • Compute: contextual sample is a 64x64 matrix sample, ~0.1 ms with Cholesky cache.

5. API design

POST /v1/bandit/decide   {surface, userCtx, candidateArms[]}
                          -> {chosenArm, propensity, decisionId}
POST /v1/bandit/reward   {decisionId, reward, ts}
GET  /v1/bandit/state/{surface}/{armId}  -> posterior params  (admin)
POST /v1/bandit/arms     {surface, armId, priorAlpha, priorBeta}

6. High-level architecture

[Client] -> [Decide API] | v [Bandit Engine] | | v v Beta-Bernoulli LinUCB (per-surface) | | +---+---+ v sample arm, log propensity p | v [Decision Log -> Kafka] \ v [Reward Joiner (Flink)] | join on decisionId v [Posterior Updater] | atomic update v [Posterior Store (Redis)] ^ | reads on every decide [Bandit Engine] ---------/

Walk-through: each request loads posterior for candidate arms, samples one (Thompson) or computes UCB, returns choice with propensity p (probability of selecting that arm). Reward arrives later, joined by decisionId in a streaming pipeline, posterior updated atomically.

7. Data model & storage

7.1 Posterior store (Redis)

KEY  bandit:{surface}:{armId}    HASH alpha,beta,n,last_update_ts
KEY  bandit:ctx:{surface}:{armId} BLOB A_inv (64x64 fp32) + b (64) for LinUCB
                                       updated via Sherman-Morrison on each reward

7.2 Decision log (Iceberg on S3)

Columns: decisionId, surface, userId, ctx[64], armId, propensity, ts.
Partitioned by date+surface. Used for off-policy evaluation and counterfactual replays.

7.3 Reward join

Flink keyed-state on decisionId, TTL 7 days. Late rewards beyond TTL discarded with metric.
Joined record sent to posterior updater and to training data lake.

8. Detailed component design

8.1 Thompson Sampling (Beta-Bernoulli)

For each candidate arm draw sample s ~ Beta(alpha, beta), pick argmax. After reward r in {0,1}: alpha += r, beta += (1-r). Naturally regret-optimal. For cold arms use weakly-informative prior Beta(1, 1) or content-derived prior.

8.2 Contextual bandit (LinUCB)

Reward expected = theta_a^T x. Maintain A_a = sum(x x^T) + lambda*I and b_a = sum(r x). At decide time: theta_hat = A_inv b; UCB = theta_hat^T x + alpha sqrt(x^T A_inv x). Cache A_inv updated via rank-1 Sherman-Morrison on each reward.

8.3 Off-policy evaluation

Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS): for new policy pi', estimated reward = mean over logs of (pi'(a|x)/p) * r. Doubly-robust adds a regression baseline. Run nightly on logs to gate launches.

9. Scaling & bottlenecks

  • Posterior staleness: 60-sec lag is fine for organic; ad-tech needs <5s -> bypass batch and update via single Flink pipeline.
  • Cold arms swamped by warm arms: use forced exploration (epsilon=0.05) or upper-confidence floor for first N pulls.
  • Non-stationarity: reward distribution drifts (creatives age out); use sliding-window posteriors or Bayesian forgetting (multiply alpha,beta by 0.99/hour).
  • Delayed rewards: conversions arrive hours later; use partial credit at click time, true-up later.
  • Hot arms: 80/20 read traffic per surface; replicate hot keys 4x in Redis.

12. Talking points for the interviewer

  • "Always log propensities. Without them you cannot do off-policy evaluation, and you'll be stuck doing online A/Bs forever."
  • "Thompson Sampling is my default. It's regret-optimal and trivial to implement. UCB is the answer when stakeholders demand explainability."
  • "Non-stationarity is the silent killer. A bandit without forgetting will lock onto stale arms; I'd add discounting from day one."

13. Tech stack & rationale

Every technology choice in this design exists for a concrete reason.

TechnologyWhy we chose it (for THIS problem)Alternatives considered
Thompson Sampling (Beta-Bernoulli) Default arm-selection: regret-optimal, trivially implementable as alpha+=r, beta+=(1-r), and natively probabilistic so propensity logging is direct. At 580K decisions/sec a single Beta sample is sub-microsecond, fitting the 10ms p99 selection budget with room for retrieval. Epsilon-greedy (wastes pulls forever); UCB (deterministic but tuning constant alpha is finicky in production).
LinUCB (contextual bandit) Stationary TS ignores user features; LinUCB with a 64-d context vector exploits user signals while keeping per-decision compute at ~0.1ms via cached Cholesky factorization. Sherman-Morrison rank-1 updates avoid recomputing A_inv on each reward. Contextual TS (similar; LinUCB picked for explainability); deep contextual (latency-prohibitive at 580K QPS).
Redis (posterior store) 20 GB total posterior state (1000 surfaces * 100K arms * 200 bytes) sub-1ms reads on every decision; HASH layout for Beta(alpha,beta,n) and BLOB for the 64x64 LinUCB matrix lets one round-trip fetch all candidate-arm state inside the 10ms decision budget. Memcached (no atomic HASH ops); DynamoDB (10ms tail breaks the 10ms p99).
Apache Kafka Decision log at 290 MB/sec (25 TB/day) and reward stream at 6 MB/sec both flow through Kafka; partitioned by decisionId so Flink's keyed-state reward join lands on the same operator that emitted the decision, no shuffle needed. Kinesis (per-shard write cap); Pulsar (less mature Flink connector).
Apache Flink (reward join + posterior update) Keyed-state on decisionId with 7-day TTL handles delayed conversions; exactly-once via Kafka transactional writes prevents lost or duplicated posterior updates per DDIA Ch.11. Sub-60s end-to-end matches the update-lag NFR. Spark Streaming (micro-batch lag); Kafka Streams (delayed-reward TTL state at this scale).
Sherman-Morrison update LinUCB matrix A_a accumulates x*x^T per reward; recomputing A_inv from scratch is O(d^3) = 260k ops, blocking the 0.1ms hot path. Sherman-Morrison rank-1 update is O(d^2) = 4k ops, fitting the latency budget while preserving exactness. Periodic refit (drifts between refreshes); Woodbury batch updates (more complex for streaming).
Apache Iceberg on S3 (decision log) 25 TB/day of decision logs partitioned by date+surface enables nightly off-policy evaluation (IPS / DR) per DDIA Ch.10; partition pruning means a typical IPS replay scans one surface-day, not the whole table. Raw Parquet on S3 (no atomic commits); Delta Lake (similar; Iceberg picked for engine neutrality).
Inverse Propensity Scoring + Doubly-Robust Counterfactually estimate a new policy without an online A/B; combined with a regression baseline (DR) reduces variance vs pure IPS. Mandatory for the launch-gate workflow given that 580K decisions/sec means a real A/B costs millions of impressions. Pure IPS (high variance with small p); pure A/B (slow and expensive launches).
Apache Spark (nightly OPE) Off-policy evaluation runs on the 25 TB/day decision log; Spark's wide-shuffle joins on (decisionId, reward) and per-policy reweighting parallelize across the cluster, completing the nightly window inside the launch-decision SLA. Beam (vendor lock); custom MapReduce (lacks the SQL-on-Iceberg ergonomics).
Bayesian forgetting (alpha,beta * 0.99/hr) Reward drifts as creatives age; multiplicative decay on (alpha, beta) every hour gives an effective sliding window of ~100 hours without explicit windowing state, the simplest production-friendly non-stationarity mitigation cited in the design. Hard sliding window (forgets useful early signal); fixed discount (less tunable).
gRPC + protobuf Per-decision payload includes 64-d context plus N candidate arm IDs; protobuf binary serialization is 4x smaller than JSON and HTTP/2 multiplexing cuts round-trip overhead, key to the 10ms decision p99. REST/JSON (verbose, slower); Thrift (similar; gRPC ecosystem stronger).
Atomic CAS posterior update Concurrent rewards for the same arm at 29K rewards/sec must not lose updates; CAS-style WATCH/MULTI/EXEC in Redis (or Lua scripts) ensures the (alpha,beta) increment is atomic per DDIA Ch.7, preventing the silent regret regression that lost-update bugs cause. Naive read-modify-write (lost updates); fully-serializable transactions (latency cost).
Decision/propensity logging Logging propensity p alongside the chosen arm at 580K decisions/sec is non-negotiable; without it IPS/DR are impossible and we'd be locked into online-only A/Bs forever, as the talking-points emphasize. Adds ~8 bytes per decision, trivial cost. No propensity (kills OPE); periodic propensity sampling (biased estimates).
