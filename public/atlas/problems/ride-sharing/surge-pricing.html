Design Surge Pricing (real-time supply-demand pricing)

Hard ride-sharing analytics

1. Problem statement

Build a real-time pricing service that computes a per-cell, per-product multiplier (1.0×, 1.5×, 2.7×, …) reflecting local supply/demand imbalance. Riders see the multiplier before they request; drivers see surge "heatmaps" to nudge them toward shortage zones. Updates must be fast enough to actually rebalance the market — typically every 60–120s.

2. Functional requirements

  • Compute surge multiplier per (city, hex_cell, product) every 60s.
  • Expose the multiplier in fare estimates and lock it for the duration of a single quote (~5 min).
  • Driver heatmap: serve a tiled overlay showing surge zones with multiplier color buckets.
  • Cap multipliers per regulatory rules (e.g., NYC max 4×, no surge during emergencies).
  • Provide an audit log: for any historical trip, why was surge X applied?

3. Non-functional requirements

  • Latency: read p99 < 30 ms (it's on the rider critical path).
  • Freshness: compute lag < 90s end-to-end.
  • Scale: ~200 cities × ~5 K cells × 4 products = 4 M keys; recomputed every 60s.
  • Stability: no flapping — apply hysteresis so a multiplier doesn't oscillate between 1.0× and 1.5× minute-by-minute.

4. Capacity estimation

  • Read QPS: 50 K fare estimates/sec + 200 K driver-heatmap polls/sec ≈ 250 K reads/sec.
  • Write QPS: 4 M keys / 60s ≈ 70 K writes/sec from the surge job.
  • Stream input: open requests (~3 K/s) + driver pings (1.25 M/s) → aggregate per-cell counters.
  • Storage: keep 90 days of per-minute snapshots = 4 M × 60 × 24 × 90 = ~518 B rows; ClickHouse compresses to ~5 TB.

5. API design

GET  /v1/surge/quote?origin=&product=                    → {quoteId, multiplier, expires}

6. High-level architecture

[Rider req stream] -+ [Driver pings] -+--> [Kafka] --> [Flink job: per-cell counters] | v [Surge Calculator] (D/S ratio + smooth) | v [Redis (per-cell)] ^ | [Rider/Driver app] -> [Surge API] --------+ | v [ClickHouse: history]

Flink maintains a 1-minute sliding window of (open_requests, idle_drivers) per cell. The Surge Calculator reads the window, applies a smoothing model, and writes the multiplier to Redis. The Surge API is a thin read-through cache. A nightly batch job replays into ClickHouse for analytics and the auditor.

7. Data model & storage

7.1 Live multipliers (Redis)

HSET surge:{city}:{cell}:{product}
     mult 1.7  cap 4.0  ts 1700000060  reason "demand>supply"
TTL 180s (so a stalled producer lets values fall back to 1.0)

7.2 Historical (ClickHouse)

CREATE TABLE surge_history (
  ts DateTime, city LowCardinality(String),
  cell FixedString(15), product LowCardinality(String),
  open_req UInt32, idle_drv UInt32, mult Float32
) ENGINE = MergeTree() PARTITION BY toYYYYMMDD(ts) ORDER BY (city, cell, ts);

7.3 Quote table (Postgres)

Ephemeral — captures the multiplier locked at quote time, used at fare-finalization to prove "the price you saw."

8. Detailed component design

8.1 Demand-supply estimator

For each cell C, compute D(C) = open ride requests in past 60s; S(C) = idle drivers currently in C plus those whose ETA to C ≤ 5 min. Raw multiplier = max(1.0, (D / max(S, 1))^α) with α tuned per product (UberX α≈0.7, Pool α≈0.5).

8.2 Smoothing & hysteresis

Apply EWMA over the last 3 minutes; require 2 consecutive minutes above the threshold before stepping up; only step up by one bucket per minute (1.0 → 1.2 → 1.4 …). Buckets are coarse — riders find continuous numbers untrustworthy.

8.3 Cap and policy layer

Pluggable rules engine: regulatory cap, "state of emergency" override, marketing campaigns ("no surge for new riders for 7 days"). Policy decisions logged for compliance.

9. Scaling & bottlenecks

  • Tiny denominator: a cell with 0 idle drivers gives infinite raw surge; clamp to bucket cap and merge with neighbor cells when supply is < 3.
  • Hot keys: Times Square gets every read; cache at the edge with 30s TTL — fresh enough for the use case.
  • Producer lag: Redis TTL of 180s ensures a stalled Flink job degrades to 1.0× instead of frozen-stale 5×.
  • Spatial granularity: H3 res 8 (~0.7 km²) — finer is noisier, coarser misses neighborhood differences.

12. Talking points for the interviewer

  • "I'd resist the urge to make this real-time — minute-level updates and coarse buckets are deliberate, because rider trust is the asset."
  • "The denominator-zero problem is the most common bug; my mitigation is neighbor-merge plus a hard regulatory cap."
  • "Surge is a market mechanism, so I'd A/B test α per city and per product, with revenue and rider conversion as the joint metric."
